◈ Critic vs Discriminator

The Critic outputs an unbounded real-valued score (no sigmoid), not a probability. Higher scores = "more real". This fundamentally changes the loss landscape — providing smooth, meaningful gradients everywhere, even when distributions don't overlap.

Discriminator: D(x) → [0, 1] (saturates)
Critic: C(x) → ℝ (no saturation)

▲ Wasserstein Distance (EMD)

Replaces JS-divergence/KL-divergence with Earth-Mover distance. EMD is continuous and differentiable almost everywhere — it doesn't go to infinity or zero when distributions don't overlap, solving the vanishing gradient problem.

W(p_r, p_g) = inf E[‖x − y‖]
Approx via Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality

◆ Gradient Penalty (GP)

WGAN-GP replaces weight clipping with a gradient penalty on interpolated samples x̂ = εx_real + (1−ε)x_fake. Penalizes the critic when its gradient norm deviates from 1, enforcing the 1-Lipschitz constraint more smoothly.

GP = λ · E[(‖∇_x̂ C(x̂)‖₂ − 1)²]
λ = 10, ε ~ Uniform(0, 1)

⚡ Training Stability

Critic is trained n_critic=5 times for every 1 Generator step. The Wasserstein loss correlates with sample quality (unlike BCE), giving a meaningful training metric. No mode collapse, no careful balancing of D vs G needed.

W-distance ↓ → sample quality ↑
Meaningful convergence metric at last!

▸ Standard GAN vs WGAN-GP

Standard GAN

  • Discriminator outputs P(real) via sigmoid
  • BCE loss (JS-divergence)
  • Vanishing gradients when D is too good
  • Mode collapse common
  • Loss doesn't correlate with quality
  • Careful D/G balance needed

WGAN-GP

  • Critic outputs unbounded score (no sigmoid)
  • Wasserstein distance (EMD)
  • Stable gradients everywhere
  • Mode collapse largely eliminated
  • Loss = meaningful quality metric
  • Train critic more (n=5), no balancing
